üîó Transition Words

Connecting Your Ideas

What Are Transition Words?

Transition words are like bridges that connect your ideas. They help your writing flow smoothly and show relationships between sentences and paragraphs.

Why Are They Important?

  • Improve the flow and style of your essay
  • Help readers follow your thoughts
  • Show how ideas relate to each other
  • Make your writing more professional

‚ö†Ô∏è Important Rule: Don't use too many transition words! Use them sparingly and selectively. Too many can make your writing cluttered and distract from your main idea.

üìã Types of Transition Words

Purpose Transition Words
Add on to an idea Likewise, Similarly, Furthermore, Moreover, Also
Contrast two ideas However, On the other hand, But, Nevertheless
Show time progression Afterward, Next, Before, Then, Finally
Prove a point Therefore, As a result, Because, Consequently
Give an example For example, In this case, For instance
Summarize In conclusion, In summary, Accordingly, Hence

üí° Transition Words in Action

Example 1: Giving an Example

"Cats have nine lives. For example, my cat fell from the window and was perfectly alright."

What it does: The phrase "For example" tells you that what follows will illustrate the previous statement.

Example 2: Adding Information

"The fall has the best weather. Furthermore, the fall has the most beautiful scenery."

What it does: "Furthermore" shows that the second sentence agrees with and adds to the first sentence.

Example 3: Contrasting Ideas

"I love Italian food, but I don't like pizza."

What it does: "But" shows a contrast between two related ideas.

‚úçÔ∏è Punctuating Transition Words

Rule 1: Punctuate Introductory Phrases

For example, I painted three pictures last week.

Why: Use a comma after the transition word when it introduces the sentence.

Rule 2: Use Comma with Conjunctions Between Independent Clauses

I wanted to watch the movie, but I had to go home for dinner.

Why: Both parts can stand alone as complete sentences, so use a comma before "but."

Rule 3: Use Commas for Transitions in the Middle

Suzy is, therefore, the best person for the job.

Why: The sentence would be complete without "therefore," so it's offset by commas.

‚ö†Ô∏è Understanding Plagiarism

What Is Plagiarism?

Plagiarism is using another person's work without giving credit to the author or presenting that work as your own.

What Must Be Acknowledged?

  • Direct quotations
  • Ideas and concepts from others
  • Statistics and data
  • Paraphrased information
  • Any information not from your own original thinking

üö® Critical Point: Even accidental plagiarism is plagiarism!

Consequences include:

  • Failing an assignment
  • Failing a course
  • Permanent notation on your academic record

‚ùå Is It Plagiarism?

Action Is It Plagiarism?
Using a quote without quotation marks or reference YES
Writing about specialized information without references YES
Changing one or two words in someone's sentence YES
Copying and pasting from a website YES
Purchasing an essay someone else wrote YES
Including citation without reference (or vice versa) YES
Submitting an essay you used in another course YES

‚úÖ How to Avoid Plagiarism

Three Essential Steps:

1. Take Notes During Research

Whenever you read an article or find information, immediately write down:

  • Author's name
  • Publication year
  • Title of the article/book
  • Website or journal name
  • Page numbers (if applicable)

2. Use In-Text Citations

When you use information from a source, include a citation in your text:

"Quotation" (Author last name, Publication Year, Page number).

3. Create a Reference List

At the end of your paper, list all sources in alphabetical order by author's last name.

üìë In-Text Citations

What Is an In-Text Citation?

A shortened reference placed in parentheses right after the quote or paraphrased information.

Format:

For a direct quote:

"Quotation text here" (Smith, 2020, p. 45).


For paraphrased information:

According to recent research, climate change affects agriculture (Jones, 2021).

What to Include:

  • Author's last name
  • Publication year
  • Page number (for direct quotes)

üí° Tip: Always use quotation marks around exact words from a source!

üìö Reference List

What Is a Reference?

A complete citation at the end of your paper that provides all information needed to find the source.

Format for Online Articles:

Smith, T. (2005). Title of article. Title of Website or Journal. Retrieved from www.website.com/Smith

Important Rules:

  • Alphabetize by author's last name
  • Use hanging indent (second line indented)
  • Include all sources cited in your paper
  • Follow APA style guidelines

Remember: Every in-text citation must have a matching reference, and every reference must be cited in your text!

üìñ Grammar: Gerunds and Infinitives

What Are They?

Gerund

A verb form ending in -ing that functions as a noun.

Swimming is good exercise.

I enjoy reading.

Infinitive

The word "to" plus the base form of a verb.

I want to study abroad.

She decided to leave early.

Key Point: When two verbs appear together, the first verb changes based on tense, but the second verb takes either the gerund or infinitive form.

‚ú® When to Use Infinitives

1. After Certain Verbs

I've decided to adopt a dog.

I want to paint you a picture.

Common verbs: want, need, decide, plan, hope, agree, promise

2. After Adjectives

It's wonderful to see you again.

It's important to put on a seatbelt when you drive.

3. After Question Words

Can you tell me how to make soup?

I don't know what to think of him.

4. To Express Purpose

I called her to tell her I love her.

She does it to annoy me.

‚ú® When to Use Gerunds

1. After Certain Verbs

I enjoy spending time with you.

She can't stand listening to rock music.

Common verbs: enjoy, finish, avoid, consider, suggest, practice, quit

2. As Subject or Object

Smoking is bad for you. (subject)

Do you think eating should be allowed in the library? (object)

3. After Prepositions

He is good at singing.

I'm looking forward to studying abroad.

Note: After prepositions (at, in, on, about, for, to), always use gerunds!

üîç Gerunds vs. Infinitives: Meaning Changes

Some verbs can take both, but the meaning changes!

Remember

remember + infinitive = you remember THEN do the action

"Did you remember to lock the door?" (Did you lock it?)


remember + gerund = you remember something from the past

"Do you remember swimming in the blue lagoon?" (past memory)

Forget

forget + infinitive = you didn't remember to do something

"I forgot to feed the fish." (I didn't feed them)


forget + gerund = you don't remember doing something in the past

"He forgot watching the movie with his girlfriend." (He did watch it but doesn't remember)

‚úçÔ∏è Written Assignment: First Draft

Writing Your Compare/Contrast Essay

What You Need:

  • Your thesis statement from Unit 4
  • Your outline from Unit 4
  • Minimum 350 words

Essay Structure:

  1. Introduction - Include your thesis statement
  2. Body Paragraphs - Develop your comparison/contrast points
  3. Conclusion - Summarize your main points

Remember to:

  • Use transition words appropriately
  • Include in-text citations if using sources
  • Proofread carefully
  • Follow APA style

üí° Essay Writing Tips

1. Follow Your Outline

Your outline is your roadmap. Each main point becomes a paragraph.

2. Start with a Strong Introduction

  • Hook the reader's attention
  • Provide background information
  • End with your thesis statement

3. Develop Body Paragraphs

  • Topic sentence for each paragraph
  • Supporting details and examples
  • Use transition words between ideas

4. Write a Strong Conclusion

  • Restate your thesis in different words
  • Summarize main points
  • End with a final thought

This is a first draft! You'll have a chance to revise it later, so focus on getting your ideas down clearly.
