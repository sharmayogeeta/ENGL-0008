Evaluating Internet Resources

Why Evaluate Sources?

The Internet provides immediate access to information, but not all information is reliable. Anyone can create a website and post information, so we must be critical consumers.

5 Key Criteria for Quality Sources:

Criteria What to Look For Red Flags
Authority Author credentials, expert background No author listed, unknown qualifications
Accuracy Facts with citations, verifiable information No sources, impossible claims
Currency Recent publication dates, updated information No dates, outdated information
Purpose Educational or informational goals Selling products, biased agenda
Objectivity Neutral language, balanced viewpoints Emotional language, one-sided

Understanding Website Domains

Domain Extensions Tell You About the Source:

.edu
Educational institutions
Usually reliable
.gov
Government bodies
Usually reliable
.org
Non-profit organizations
Often reliable
.com
Commercial enterprises
Evaluate carefully

Red Flags to Avoid:

  • Anonymous authors: No name or credentials listed
  • No citations: Claims without supporting evidence
  • Outdated information: No recent update dates
  • Bias language: Emotional or inflammatory words
  • Poor writing: Grammar errors, informal style
  • Wikipedia: Never use as a primary source in academic writing

Best Practice: Use academic databases from your university library whenever possible!

What is a Thesis Statement?

Think of it Like a Movie Trailer

A thesis statement is like a movie trailer for your essay - it tells readers what to expect and makes them want to keep reading!

Key Characteristics:

  • Location: Usually found in the introduction paragraph
  • Length: Typically 1-2 sentences
  • Purpose: States your main argument or point
  • Preview: Shows what the rest of your essay will discuss

❌ Weak Thesis

"Telecommuting is an option for some people who can work on their computer."

Problem: Just states a fact, no clear opinion

✅ Strong Thesis

"Telecommuting seems great because it offers flexibility, but it only works for people who have self-discipline, independence, and a willingness to maintain a home office."

Strength: Clear opinion with specific supporting points

The Thesis Statement Checklist

Ask Yourself These 5 Questions:

1. Is your opinion clear?

Your thesis should take a position, not just state facts.

Weak: "Many students use online learning."
Strong: "Online learning is more effective than traditional classroom learning for working adults."

2. Can someone disagree?

A good thesis presents an arguable point, not a universally accepted fact.

3. Is it specific?

Provide concrete details about what you'll discuss.

4. Where are you going?

Give readers a roadmap of your main points.

5. Have you revised?

Thesis statements should evolve as you research and write.

How Thesis Statements Evolve

Example: From Weak to Strong

Step 1: Initial Idea

"Telecommuting seems great, but it is not for everyone."

Problem: Too vague - what's great about it? Why not for everyone?

Step 2: Adding Details

"Telecommuting seems great because it offers flexibility, but it only works for people who have self-discipline, independence, and a willingness to maintain a home office."

Better: Specific reasons why it's great and who it works for

Step 3: Final Polish

"Whether someone views telecommuting as beneficial or challenging depends on his or her self-discipline, independence, and willingness to maintain a home office."

Strongest: Clear structure for compare-contrast essay

Pro Tips:

  • Start with a working thesis - it will change!
  • Take breaks and return with fresh eyes
  • Each highlighted point becomes a body paragraph
  • Make sure your thesis matches your essay type

Developing Essay Outlines

Why Outline?

  • Organize your thoughts before writing
  • Ensure logical flow of ideas
  • Prevent writer's block
  • Keep focused on your main argument

Basic Outline Structure:

I. Introduction
    A. Hook/attention grabber
    B. Background information
    C. Thesis statement

II. Body Paragraph 1
    A. Topic sentence (first main point)
    B. Supporting evidence
    C. Analysis/explanation

III. Body Paragraph 2
    A. Topic sentence (second main point)
    B. Supporting evidence
    C. Analysis/explanation

IV. Body Paragraph 3
    A. Topic sentence (third main point)
    B. Supporting evidence
    C. Analysis/explanation

V. Conclusion
    A. Restate thesis
    B. Summarize main points
    C. Final thought/call to action

Conjunctions: Joining Ideas

What are Conjunctions?

Conjunctions are "joining words" that connect words, phrases, or clauses. Think of them as connectors between train cars!

Two Main Types:

Coordinating Conjunctions

Join equal parts (words, phrases, or independent clauses)

AND
BUT
OR
NOR
FOR
YET
SO

Subordinating Conjunctions

Connect dependent clauses to independent clauses

AFTER
ALTHOUGH
BECAUSE
BEFORE
IF
SINCE
WHEN
WHILE

Using Conjunctions Correctly

Coordinating Conjunction Examples:

  • AND: "I like ice cream and chocolate chip cookies."
  • BUT: "I wanted to drive to the party, but Sally insisted we walk."
  • SO: "I had a test this morning, so I went to bed early."

Note: Use a comma before coordinating conjunctions when joining two complete sentences!

Subordinating Conjunction Examples:

  • WHEN: "Clara screamed when she saw the spider."
  • BECAUSE: "I have a stomachache because I ate too much ice cream."
  • ALTHOUGH: "Although it was raining, we went for a walk."

Note: Use a comma when the subordinating clause comes first!

Practice:

Fill in the blanks: "You won't pass your class _____ you study hard."
Answer: unless (subordinating conjunction)

Countable vs. Uncountable Nouns

Understanding the Difference:

Countable Nouns Uncountable Nouns
Things you can count individually Things you cannot count individually
Can be singular or plural Always singular
Use a/an with singular forms Never use a/an
Examples:
one apple → two apples
a chair → three chairs
a student → many students		 Examples:
water (not waters)
information (not informations)
advice (not advices)

Using Articles and Quantifiers:

Countable Uncountable
Positive a banana / some bananas some flour
Negative not an egg / not any eggs not any milk
Questions How many? / any books? How much? / any water?

Common Uncountable Nouns to Remember

Academic Writing Vocabulary:

information
research
advice
homework
progress
equipment
furniture
luggage
traffic
weather

Tricky Examples - Same Word, Different Meanings:

  • Paper (uncountable) = material for writing
    A paper (countable) = a research document
  • Glass (uncountable) = material
    A glass (countable) = drinking container
  • Ice cream (uncountable) = the food
    An ice cream (countable) = one serving

Memory Tip:

When in doubt, think: "Can I put a number in front of it naturally?" If yes, it's probably countable!

Written Assignment: Oral Presentation

🎥 Create a Short Oral Presentation

Present your compare-contrast essay topic to your classmates using video/audio recording.

Answer These 5 Questions:

  1. What is your topic? (State it clearly)
  2. Why did you select this topic? (Personal interest? Relevance?)
  3. What are you comparing/contrasting? (Specific subjects)
  4. What criteria will you use? (Basis for comparison)
  5. What's your main point? (What should readers learn?)

📋 Step-by-Step Process:

  1. Write out your answers first
  2. Review for errors and clarity
  3. Practice speaking clearly and slowly
  4. Record using Kaltura, Loom, or ScreenPal
  5. Upload your recording

Speaking Tips:

  • Speak slowly and clearly
  • Make eye contact with the camera
  • Use natural gestures
  • Practice beforehand
  • Keep it conversational but organized

Test Your Understanding

Answer These Questions:

Question 1:

Which domain extension is typically most reliable for academic research?

a) .com     b) .edu     c) .net     d) .info

Question 2:

Complete this sentence with the correct conjunction:
"I wanted to study abroad, _____ my parents were worried about the cost."

Question 3:

Which is correct?

a) "I need some advices about my essay."
b) "I need some advice about my essay."

Question 4:

What makes a thesis statement strong? List three characteristics.

Question 5:

Name three criteria you should use to evaluate Internet sources.

Answers

Answer 1:

b) .edu - Educational institutions are typically most reliable for academic research, followed by .gov (government) and .org (non-profit organizations).

Answer 2:

"but" - This is a coordinating conjunction showing contrast between two independent clauses. Don't forget the comma!

Answer 3:

b) "I need some advice about my essay." - "Advice" is uncountable, so it cannot be plural. We use "some" with uncountable nouns.

Answer 4:

A strong thesis statement:

  • Presents a clear, specific opinion
  • Is arguable (someone could disagree)
  • Provides a roadmap for the essay
  • Is focused and not too broad
  • Appears in the introduction

Answer 5:

Criteria for evaluating sources:

  • Authority: Who wrote it? What are their credentials?
  • Accuracy: Are facts supported with citations?
  • Currency: Is the information current and updated?
  • Purpose: Why was this created? Is it educational?
  • Objectivity: Is the language neutral and unbiased?
