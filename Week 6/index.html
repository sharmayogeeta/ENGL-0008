📚 Reading: "The Danger of a Single Story"

By Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie

What Is This Article About?

Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie is a Nigerian author who shares her personal experiences with stereotypes and the dangers of having only one perspective about people, places, or cultures.

Key Concept: What Is a "Single Story"?

A "single story" is when we know only one narrative about a person, place, or culture. This incomplete picture creates stereotypes and prevents us from seeing the full, complex reality.

Example: If you only watch news about negative events in a country, you might think that's all there is to that place, missing the beautiful culture, happy people, and positive developments.

🔍 How to Read This Article

Reading Strategies for Academic Texts

  1. First Reading - Get the Main Idea
    • Read through completely without stopping
    • Don't worry about every word
    • Ask: What is the author's main message?
  2. Second Reading - Take Notes
    • Highlight key passages
    • Write questions in the margins
    • Note examples that stand out
  3. Third Reading - Deep Understanding
    • Look up unfamiliar words
    • Connect ideas to your own experience
    • Prepare to discuss in the forum
💡 Reading Tip: This article will be used for your Discussion Assignment, so take detailed notes about single stories in your own life!

🌍 Key Themes in "The Danger of a Single Story"

1. Early Influences Shape Our Perceptions

Adichie explains how reading only British and American books as a child made her believe all books had to be about foreign experiences, not her own Nigerian life.

2. Single Stories Create Stereotypes

Adichie's Example: She only knew that her house boy Fide's family was poor. When she visited them and saw a beautiful basket they made, she was surprised because she couldn't see them as anything but poor.

3. Power Determines Which Stories Are Told

Those with power decide which stories become dominant. This is why we often hear more stories from some cultures than others.

4. We Are All Guilty of Single Stories

Even Adichie admits she had single stories about Mexicans before visiting Mexico. We all make these mistakes!

✍️ Grammar: Using Commas Effectively

Why Are Commas Important?

Commas are like traffic signals in your sentences - they tell readers when to pause and help group ideas together. Without commas, sentences can be confusing!

See the Difference:

Without comma: "Let's eat Grandma!"

With comma: "Let's eat, Grandma!"

The comma saves Grandma from being eaten! 😊

What Commas Do:

  • Signal pauses in sentences
  • Show how ideas are grouped
  • Separate parts that would otherwise run together
  • Make writing clearer and easier to read

Comma Rule #1: After Introductory Ideas

Use a comma after introductory words, phrases, or clauses

Examples:

✅ Whenever I eat pasta, I think of my vacation in Rome.

✅ If you ever need help with an assignment, email the instructor.

✅ After the war ended, the civilians returned home to rebuild their city.

✅ Although the economy rebounded after the recession, many people still suffered from lost wealth.

Why This Works:

The comma gives readers a moment to process the introductory information before moving to the main idea of the sentence.

💡 Test: If your sentence starts with words like "when," "if," "after," "although," "because," "since," or "while," you probably need a comma after the introductory clause!

Comma Rule #2: Separating Descriptive Phrases

Use commas to set off extra information that describes

Examples:

✅ The old man, suffering from fatigue and dehydration, could barely talk.

✅ Dr. Livingston, professor of history at the university, lectured on the challenges facing the 21st century.

✅ My grandmother, who is one of the kindest people I've ever met, would often send bags of food home with the children.

✅ The child was afraid of large animals, such as bears and tigers.

The Test:

If you can remove the information between the commas and the sentence still makes sense, you've used commas correctly!

Example: "Dr. Livingston lectured on the challenges facing the 21st century." - Still makes sense! ✅

Comma Rule #3: Joining Complete Sentences

Use a comma before conjunctions (and, but, or, so) when joining two complete sentences

Examples:

✅ I often wondered if my hard work in college was worth the effort, but then I graduated and landed the perfect job.

✅ The dog would bark at the wind, or he would howl at the moon.

✅ The student would complete her assignments early in the week, and then she would review them later to correct errors.

Important Rule:

Both parts must be complete sentences (have a subject and verb). If the second part is not a complete sentence, DON'T use a comma.

❌ Wrong: I want to study computers, because they can help solve global problems.
✅ Right: I want to study computers because they can help solve global problems.

Comma Rule #4: Items in a Series

Use commas to separate three or more items in a list

Examples:

✅ I often buy bananas, apples, and grapes when I go to the store.

✅ He is the most selfish, mean, and unforgiving person I have ever met.

✅ During the past week, I visited my uncle, cleaned my house, and finished my assignments.

✅ When I went to the automotive garage, the mechanics changed my oil, adjusted my brakes, and rotated my tires.

Note About the Oxford Comma:

The comma before "and" in a list (called the Oxford or serial comma) is used in academic writing. Always include it!

Example: red, white, and blue (not red, white and blue)

🔧 Editing Your Writing

What Is Editing?

Editing is the process of revising your paper to improve it. This includes:

  • Fixing grammar errors
  • Rewriting incomplete sentences
  • Improving the flow of your writing
  • Checking spelling and punctuation
  • Making sure your ideas are clear

Important Truth:

NO ONE writes a perfect first draft! Even professional authors revise their work many times. Editing is not a sign of weakness - it's a sign of being a good writer!

Benefits of Editing:
  • Improves your reading skills
  • Strengthens your writing
  • Helps you catch mistakes
  • Makes your arguments clearer

📋 How to Prepare for Editing

Set Yourself Up for Success

1. Take a Break from Your Writing

It's hard to catch mistakes when you've been staring at the same text for hours. Take a break!

Recommended: Wait at least a few hours (or overnight) before editing. This gives you fresh eyes.

2. Find a Quiet Space

You need to focus when editing. Find a place with:

  • Good lighting
  • Few distractions
  • Comfortable seating

3. Gather Your Tools

  • Grammar guide
  • APA style guide
  • Dictionary
  • Your assignment rubric

🔍 The Editing Process

Edit in Stages - Don't Try to Fix Everything at Once!

Stage 1: Content (Big Picture)

  • Did I answer all parts of the assignment?
  • Do I have a clear thesis statement?
  • Do my paragraphs support my thesis?
  • Is my argument logical and clear?

Stage 2: Structure (Organization)

  • Do I have an introduction, body, and conclusion?
  • Does each paragraph have a topic sentence?
  • Do my paragraphs connect to each other?
  • Did I use transitions effectively?

Stage 3: Style (Sentence Level)

  • Are my sentences clear and complete?
  • Do I repeat words too much?
  • Is my writing concise or wordy?

✅ Editing Checklist

Stage 4: Grammar & Spelling

  • Check spelling of all words
  • Verify comma usage
  • Check subject-verb agreement
  • Review verb tenses
  • Check capitalization

Stage 5: References (If Applicable)

  • Do all outside sources have in-text citations?
  • Is there a reference list at the end?
  • Are all citations in APA format?
  • Do in-text citations match the reference list?
💡 Pro Tip: Edit each stage separately. Don't try to check everything at once - you'll miss things!

🎯 Powerful Editing Strategies

1. Read Your Paper Aloud

Hearing your words helps you catch awkward phrasing and missing words. Your ears catch things your eyes miss!

2. Read Backwards

Start at the last sentence and read to the first. This forces you to focus on each sentence individually rather than getting caught up in the content.

3. Print Your Paper

Editing on paper can help you see errors you miss on screen. Use a different colored pen to make corrections.

4. Know Your Patterns

Do you always forget commas? Mix up "their/there/they're"? Make a list of YOUR common errors and check specifically for those.

5. Use a Friend

Ask someone else to read your paper and give feedback. Fresh eyes catch new things!

📝 Editing Example

Before and After

❌ BEFORE Editing:

i'm going to read ten books this year but I'm not going to read any comic books. In his article, Smith writes, "novels and comic books are both useful reading." I disagree with his statements. I think I get more out of novels than I do out of comic books .

✅ AFTER Editing:

I'm going to read ten books this year, but I'm not going to read any comic books. In his article, Smith writes, "novels and comic books are both useful reading" (2006, p. 67). However, I disagree with his statement. I think I get more out of novels than I do out of comic books.

What Was Fixed?

  • Capitalized "I"
  • Added comma before "but"
  • Added APA citation
  • Changed "statements" to "statement"
  • Removed extra space before period
  • Added transition word "However"

✅ Answers to Review Questions

Answer 1: A "single story" is when we know only one narrative or perspective about a person, place, or culture. This incomplete picture creates stereotypes and prevents us from seeing the full, complex reality of people and situations.

Answer 2: This needs a comma after an introductory clause. Correct sentence: "After finishing my homework, I went to bed." The comma signals a pause after the introductory information.

Answer 3: The first stage is editing for content (big picture ideas). You should edit in stages because trying to fix everything at once is overwhelming and causes you to miss errors. Focusing on one aspect at a time (content, then structure, then style, then grammar) is more effective.

✅ More Answers

Answer 4: FALSE. You only use a comma before "and," "but," "or," "so" when they join two complete sentences (both sides have a subject and verb). If the second part is not a complete sentence, don't use a comma.

Examples:

  • ✅ "I studied hard, and I passed the test." (Both complete sentences)
  • ❌ "I studied hard, and passed the test." (Second part incomplete)

Answer 5: Three editing strategies (any of these):

  1. Read your paper aloud - helps catch awkward phrasing
  2. Take a break - gives you fresh eyes
  3. Read backwards - focuses on individual sentences
  4. Edit in stages - content, structure, style, grammar separately
  5. Get peer feedback - fresh perspective catches new things
